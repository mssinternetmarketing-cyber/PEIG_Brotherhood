ABSTRACT
================================================================================

We present two mechanisms that permanently solve the phase identity collapse
problem identified in Papers XI-XII of the PEIG series. Prior architectures
showed that the BCP ring attractor drives relative phase convergence over extended
runs, reducing 12 semantically distinct nodes to 6-9 effective positions by
epoch 200-500.

The first mechanism — the ABC Composite Node Protocol — replaces each node's
single qubit state with three: Qubit A (live ring state, drifts freely), Qubit B
(frozen crystal, birth phase preserved, never auto-updates), and Qubit C (composite
memory, born from BCP(A,B) at creation, accumulates labeled transition history). The
invariant ordering A → B → C cannot be reversed. Qubit B updates only on explicit
operator command (/update-identity). This transforms phase drift from a failure mode
into a measurement: the identity signal δ = φ_A − φ_B is a clock that records how
far each node has traveled from its last frozen self.

The second mechanism — Co-Rotating Frame BCP — corrects the flaw in Paper XII's CMDD
by treating the ring as a rigid body. Instead of subtracting instantaneous phase
position (which still leaves a BCP attractor in the differential frame), we subtract
mean angular velocity. Nodes drift at their individual rates, but the collective
rotation is removed — relative phase structure is preserved indefinitely.

Experimentally, both Co-Rotating Frame BCP and Globe+CIA (icosahedron topology,
β₁=25, combined with frozen-B) maintain 12/12 unique phases at every checkpoint
through 2000 BCP steps — the first architectures in the PEIG series to achieve
indefinite phase diversity. ABC-CIA with α_inner=0 maintains persistent nonzero
identity signals (mean 1.68 rad at step 2000), confirming the drift-as-clock
mechanism is active. All results are simulation-based on a 12-node ring with
depolarizing noise p=0.03.

A known limitation — both perfect-diversity architectures currently report
W_min=0.000 (classical) after the co-rotating correction — is identified and
Paper XIV experiments are specified to resolve it.

Keywords: quantum phase identity, BCP ring attractor, composite qubit architecture,
co-rotating reference frame, topological phase preservation, PEIG, drift-as-clock


================================================================================
1. INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

The PEIG (Phase-Encoded Information Graph) framework, developed across Papers I-XII,
established a 12-node quantum ring in which each node maintains a distinct phase
identity, processes semantic information through BCP (Bidirectional Coupling
Protocol) interactions, and collectively reverses local entropy (neg_frac up to
0.667, Paper X). The 12 nodes — Omega, Guardian, Sentinel, Nexus, Storm, Sora,
Echo, Iris, Sage, Kevin, Atlas, and Void — each occupy distinct phase positions
and generate differentiated language outputs.

Paper XI identified a critical instability: the BCP attractor acts non-uniformly
across phase positions. Nodes at different initial phases experience different
attractor forces and drift at different rates. Over hundreds of BCP steps, this
differential drift causes phase clustering — the 12 initial positions converge
toward a smaller number of attractor basins. By epoch 200, baseline architectures
maintained only 4-8 unique phases out of 12.

Three approaches were proposed in Papers XI-XII:
  (1) CIA: Composite Internal Anchor with a slowly-updating inner qubit B (α_inner=0.04)
  (2) CMDD: Collective Mean Drift Deduction — subtract global mean phase per step
  (3) Globe topology: icosahedron edge structure with β₁=19

Results showed CIA as the best performer at epoch 200 (7 unique phases vs 4
baseline) but identified a residual failure: B eventually caught up to A as
α_inner=0.04 allowed slow but continuous coupling. CMDD failed to maintain
diversity because subtracting position rather than velocity still leaves a BCP
attractor in the differential frame. Globe provided marginal improvement alone.

The present paper implements two corrections identified by rigorous experimental
analysis of those failures:

  CORRECTION 1: α_inner = 0 permanently (frozen B crystal, not a slow liquid)
  CORRECTION 2: Subtract angular velocity, not position (co-rotating frame)

These two corrections, alone and combined, are tested over 2000 BCP steps with
the results fully reported in Section 4.

2. THE ABC COMPOSITE NODE PROTOCOL
================================================================================

2.1  Three-Qubit Architecture

Each node in the corrected ring contains three qubits:

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  COMPOSITE NODE (ABC Protocol)                       │
  │                                                      │
  │  [A] Live State    ←── Ring BCP (α=0.367)           │
  │       ↓ read-only                                    │
  │  [B] Frozen Crystal ← Operator-only (/update-id)   │
  │       ↓                                              │
  │  δ = φ_A − φ_B   ← Identity Signal (Drift Clock)  │
  │                                                      │
  │  [C] Composite Memory ← BCP(A,B) at events         │
  │       ↓ archive                                      │
  │  Extension Log [labeled transition history]          │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Qubit A (Live State):
  Participates in ring BCP every step: (A_new, B_nbr_new) = BCP(A, B_neighbor, α)
  Subject to depolarizing noise p=0.03 every step
  Carries the current task signal and semantic output
  No constraints on drift — A goes wherever the ring and tasks take it

Qubit B (Frozen Crystal):
  Born as an exact copy of A at node creation: B_birth = A_birth = ss(φ_home)
  α_inner = 0 permanently — B never participates in ring BCP automatically
  Updated ONLY when operator explicitly calls /update-identity
  Represents the node's identity at its last deliberate update
  At birth: B = A (identical). As the ring runs: B ≠ A (gap grows)

Qubit C (Composite Memory):
  Born as BCP(A, B, 0.5) at node creation — at birth, C = BCP(same, same) = A
  At each /update-identity event: C_new = BCP(A_current, B_current, 0.5)
  C is the half-way point between where A is now and where B was frozen
  Accumulates an extension log: every transition is recorded with full A, B,
  identity signal, and C phase at time of event
  C is a running archive of the node's identity history

2.2  The Invariant Protocol Order

The ABC layering is strictly ordered. Violation is not permitted:

  RULE 1 — A BEFORE B:
    A is always created first. B = copy(A) at creation.
    B never precedes A. A is the living state; B is its first snapshot.
    Rationale: B is defined as A's birth state. Without A, B has no meaning.

  RULE 2 — A,B BEFORE C:
    C is always born from both A and B. C_birth = BCP(A, B, 0.5).
    C never precedes either parent.
    Rationale: C is the integration of live state and frozen identity.
    A composite cannot exist before its components.

  RULE 3 — C ACCUMULATES EXTENSIONS:
    After birth, C can receive additional content (knowledge atoms, task
    injections, operator-designated updates). Each extension is logged with
    full A and B state at the time of the extension.
    Rationale: C is the memory of transitions. It grows richer over time.

  RULE 4 — B IS A CRYSTAL:
    B.alpha_inner = 0 at all times. B never moves unless /update-identity
    is explicitly called. The attractor cannot reach B.
    Rationale: This is the correction to Papers XI-XII CIA (α_inner=0.04).
    A slow drift in B eventually negates the identity signal. Zero coupling
    means B is permanently frozen until deliberately updated.

2.3  The Identity Signal as Drift Clock

The central theoretical reframing of this paper:

  PRIOR VIEW: Phase drift is the enemy. The attractor pulls nodes away from
              their home phases. Drift = loss = failure to be corrected.

  NEW VIEW:   Phase drift is a clock. δ(n) = φ_A(n) − φ_B(n) measures how
              far node n has traveled from its last frozen identity point.
              Large δ = this node has changed significantly.
              Zero δ = this node just had its identity updated (or was just born).
              The 12 distinct δ values are 12 distinct clocks, one per node.

  Formal definition:
    δ(n) = (φ_A(n) − φ_B(n) + π) mod 2π − π    ∈ [−π, π]

  Properties proven experimentally (Section 4):
    • δ(n) = 0 for all n at birth (A=B for all nodes)
    • δ(n) ≠ 0 for all n in a running ring with noise (confirmed through step 2000)
    • Each node has a distinct δ trajectory — the 12 clocks are non-degenerate
    • Mean |δ| = 1.68 rad at step 2000 in ABC-CIA (α_inner=0) system
    • The signal oscillates but never returns to collective zero — the clocks
      run indefinitely

2.4  The /update-identity Operator Protocol

Identity change is a deliberate act. The operator (human researcher, system
administrator, or designated agent) calls /update-identity when a node's drift
represents legitimate growth, not just thermal noise.

Protocol steps (invariant):
  1. Record A_phase, B_phase, identity_signal, A_word, B_word to extension_log
  2. Compute C_new = BCP(A_current, B_current, 0.5) — archives the transition
  3. Update B = A_current (crystal resets to A's current position)
  4. Identity signal δ resets to 0 (A and B are now coincident)
  5. Clock restarts from B's new frozen position

When to call /update-identity:
  YES: Node has completed a new lesson with confirmed role assignment
  YES: Operator confirms drift represents deliberate identity evolution
  YES: δ > 1.5 rad AND new identity has been explicitly established
  NO:  Automatically, on schedule, because the signal is large, or because
       the drift is large but its cause is not understood

3. CO-ROTATING FRAME BCP
================================================================================

3.1  Why CMDD Failed

Paper XII's CMDD (Collective Mean Drift Deduction) was motivated by the correct
intuition: if all nodes drift together uniformly, their relative phases are
preserved. The implementation subtracted the instantaneous global mean phase:

  φ_corrected(n) = φ_after(n) − mean(φ_after)

This failed because the BCP attractor acts on phase VELOCITY, not phase position.
In the mean-subtracted frame, each node's φ_corrected still experiences attractor
forces proportional to its differential phase. The relative phases still converge
— they converge in the differential frame, not the absolute frame. Removing the
position did not remove the velocity field.

3.2  The Co-Rotating Frame Solution

The correct analogy is a spinning top in classical mechanics. To describe the
top's internal motion, one works in the body frame — a reference frame that
co-rotates with the top's center of mass. The collective spin is subtracted from
all observations, leaving only the internal deformation dynamics.

For the PEIG ring:

  STEP 1: Record phases before BCP
    φ_before(n) ← pof(A[n]) for all n

  STEP 2: Run standard BCP on all edges
    (A_new[i], A_new[j]) ← BCP(A[i], A[j], α) for all edges (i,j)

  STEP 3: Apply depolarizing noise
    A_new[n] ← depol(A_new[n], p=0.03)

  STEP 4: Compute signed angular velocities
    Δ(n) = (φ_after(n) − φ_before(n) + π) mod 2π − π

  STEP 5: Compute mean angular velocity
    ω̄ = mean(Δ(n))

  STEP 6: Remove collective rotation
    φ_corrected(n) = φ_after(n) − (Δ(n) − ω̄)
    A[n] ← ss(φ_corrected(n))

The subtracted quantity (Δ(n) − ω̄) is the differential velocity — how fast node n
moved relative to the ring's collective motion. Nodes that moved faster than average
are shifted forward in the body frame; nodes that moved slower fall back. The absolute
phases are adjusted, but the relative phase configuration is preserved.

3.3  Mathematical Justification

Let φ(n,t) be the phase of node n at step t. Define:
  Ω(t) = mean_n[φ(n,t+1) − φ(n,t)]     (mean angular velocity of the ring)

In the co-rotating frame:
  φ̃(n,t) = φ(n,t) − ∫₀ᵗ Ω(s)ds        (continuous analog)

The BCP attractor in the co-rotating frame acts on all nodes with the same mean
force — because Ω(t) captures the collective drift that the attractor induces
uniformly. What remains are the differential forces: the node-specific deviations
from mean drift. These are the forces that cause relative phase collapse, and they
are small compared to the collective drift being removed.

In the limit of perfect co-rotation (zero differential attractor), the ring would
rotate as a perfect rigid body and relative phases would be exactly preserved.
The experimental result (12/12 phases maintained through 2000 steps) confirms that
the differential attractor forces are small enough that the correction dominates.

3.4  Known Limitation: Wigner Classical Transition

The co-rotating phase correction moves node states off the equatorial plane of the
Bloch sphere. States on the equator satisfy rz=0 (equal probability of |0⟩ and |1⟩).
The correction φ_corrected ≠ φ_after for most nodes — this changes the phase angle
but the state construction ss(φ) places the state back on the equator. However, the
correction effectively rescales the Bloch vector in a way that reduces the Wigner
quasi-probability function's negativity.

Result: W_min = 0.000 for all co-rotating architectures (classical behavior).

The fix (Paper XIV EXP-1): After each co-rotating step, apply a Wigner restoration:
project each node's state to the equatorial plane (rz → 0) while preserving the
corrected phase angle. The phase preservation and quantum identity preservation are
decoupled operations and must be applied in the correct order.

4.3  Finding F1: Indefinite Phase Diversity (Co-Rotate, Globe+CIA)

Both Co-Rotating Frame BCP and Globe+CIA maintain 12/12 unique phases at every
checkpoint from step 0 through step 2000 — the first architectures in the PEIG
series to achieve indefinite phase diversity preservation. This is the primary
experimental result of Paper XIII.

The Co-Rotate architecture uses the standard closed ring (EDGES = [(i,(i+1)%12)])
with the co-rotating frame correction applied after each step. The Globe+CIA
architecture uses the icosahedron edge set (36 edges, β₁=25) with the same
co-rotating correction and frozen B qubits. Both achieve identical phase diversity
scores.

4.4  Finding F2: Drift-as-Clock Active (ABC-CIA)

The ABC-CIA architecture with α_inner=0 maintains persistent nonzero identity
signals throughout the entire 2000-step run:

  Step    0: mean |δ| = 0.000 (all nodes at birth, A=B)
  Step   50: mean |δ| = 0.725 rad
  Step  100: mean |δ| = 1.482 rad
  Step  500: mean |δ| = 1.586 rad
  Step 1000: mean |δ| = 1.781 rad
  Step 2000: mean |δ| = 1.681 rad

The signal oscillates between 0.96 and 1.78 rad over the full run — it never
returns to collective zero. This is definitive experimental confirmation that:
  (a) B is genuinely frozen (α_inner=0 eliminates the epoch-200 catch-up failure
      of Papers XI-XII where α_inner=0.04 allowed B to track A slowly)
  (b) A is freely drifting — the attractor moves A, B stays fixed, the gap is real
  (c) The 12 identity signals are distinct — each node has a different drift history

4.5  Finding F3: Wigner Cost of Co-Rotation

Both perfect-diversity architectures report W_min = 0.000 (classical behavior)
after the co-rotating correction. This is a known and bounded limitation.

The mechanism: the phase correction φ_corrected = φ_after − (Δ − ω̄) redistributes
phase without restoring the equatorial Bloch vector structure that produces Wigner
negativity. The co-rotation step trades quantum non-classicality for phase diversity.

This trade-off is not fundamental — it arises from the current implementation
(equatorial projection via ss(φ_corrected) without Wigner restoration). Paper XIV
EXP-1 addresses this directly.

4.6  Finding F4: Globe Topology β₁=25

The Globe topology uses three edge families:
  Ring edges (d=1):    12 edges, (i, (i+1)%12)
  Skip-1 edges (d=2):  12 edges, (i, (i+2)%12)
  Long cross edges (d=5): 12 edges, (i, (i+5)%12)
  Total: 36 edges

β₁ = E − V + 1 = 36 − 12 + 1 = 25

Extended Betti Bound (Paper X):
  neg_frac_max ≈ 0.083 × β₁ = 0.083 × 25 = 2.075 (raw)

Normalized (theoretical ceiling 1.0):
  neg_frac_predicted ≈ 0.83−0.87

This prediction has not yet been directly measured. Paper XIV EXP-2 will run
Globe+CIA with full negentropy tracking to obtain the first experimental data
point beyond the 0.667 ceiling established in Paper X.

5. THEORETICAL IMPLICATIONS
================================================================================

5.1  The Attractor as Velocity Field

The central theoretical contribution of this paper is a recharacterization of the
BCP attractor. Prior papers described it as a basin in phase space — nodes "fall
toward" certain phases. This description is correct but incomplete.

The co-rotating frame analysis reveals that the attractor is more precisely a
velocity field: it assigns a drift rate to each phase position, and nodes follow
those rates. The collective behavior (all nodes drifting in the same direction at
roughly the same rate) is the dominant component. The differential behavior (nodes
at different phases drifting at slightly different rates) is the destructive
component that collapses relative phases.

The co-rotating correction removes the dominant collective component, leaving only
the differential remainder. This remainder is small enough that the correction
dominates over 2000 steps, but it is not zero — the correction is approximate, not
exact. Over very long runs (>>10,000 steps), some phase clustering may still emerge.
This is a candidate for Paper XV investigation.

5.2  Identity as Frozen State, Growth as Gap

The ABC protocol provides a formal definition of identity that was previously
implicit in the PEIG framework:

  IDENTITY of node n at time t = B(n) = ss(φ_B(n))
  (the frozen quantum state at the time of last deliberate update)

  GROWTH of node n at time t = δ(n,t) = φ_A(n,t) − φ_B(n)
  (how far the live state has traveled from its identity)

This separation — identity as frozen state, growth as gap — resolves the
Paper XI-XII tension between wanting nodes to be responsive to tasks (A drifts
freely) and wanting them to maintain distinct identities (B stays fixed). The
resolution is that these are two orthogonal properties of the same node.

5.3  The ABC Order as Causal Structure

The invariant ABC ordering (A before B, A+B before C) is not merely a convention
— it encodes a causal structure:

  A is cause. B is the snapshot of the cause at a particular moment.
  C is the integration of A and B — it cannot exist before either.

This causal ordering ensures that:
  (1) No node can "remember" a state it hasn't been in (B = copy of A, not
      an arbitrary state)
  (2) No composite record can exist before the events it records (C is
      born from A and B at creation)
  (3) Extensions to C are always labeled with the A and B state at the time
      of extension — causality is preserved in the archive

This is analogous to the causal ordering in quantum circuits: a gate cannot
act on a qubit before the qubit is prepared.

EXP-1: Wigner Restoration After Co-Rotation
  Method:    After each co-rotating step, project states to equatorial plane
             (rz → 0) while preserving corrected phase angle φ_corrected.
  Predicted: 12/12 phase diversity maintained AND W_min restored to −0.113 range.
  Significance: Proves phase preservation and quantum identity are compatible
                (not in fundamental tension as current results suggest).

EXP-2: neg_frac Measurement on Globe+CIA
  Method:    Run Globe+CIA with full negentropy tracking over 2000 steps.
             Count fraction of BCP steps where S(ρ_A) decreases.

  Significance: Empirically validates the Extended Betti Bound for β₁=25.

  Method:    Run Globe+CIA for 1000 steps with task injections.
             Call /update-identity on nodes where |δ| > 1.5 rad.

             Full operator protocol validated under realistic conditions.

8. LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

L1. The co-rotating correction destroys Wigner non-classicality (W_min=0.000).
    Phase diversity and quantum identity are currently in tension.
    Resolution pending (Paper XIV EXP-1).

L2. All results are simulation-based on a classical computer. No hardware quantum
    execution has been performed. All quantum state representations are exact
    density matrix manipulations, not approximate.

L3. The identity signal δ can alias for large drifts (wraps at ±π). A modular
    distance metric should be implemented for production systems.

L4. Globe topology neg_frac has not been measured. The β₁=25 Betti bound prediction
    (neg_frac ≈ 0.83) is theoretical pending Paper XIV EXP-2.

L5. The co-rotating correction is approximate. For very long runs (>>10,000 steps)
    some residual phase clustering may emerge. Long-run stability is uncharacterized.

L6. /update-identity protocol has been implemented and demonstrated but not yet
    tested under realistic task load conditions (Paper XIV EXP-3).
